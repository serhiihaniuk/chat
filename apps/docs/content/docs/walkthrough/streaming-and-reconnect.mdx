Maya's three Jira tickets are halfway through typing themselves out when she snaps her laptop shut to
run to a meeting. She reopens it twenty minutes later, clicks back to the chat, and the full answer
is sitting there — finished. Nothing was lost, nothing was re-asked. The same trick works when the
company grows and runs **three** copies of the server behind a load balancer: the request that
started Maya's turn and the request that reconnects to it can land on two different machines, and it
still just works.

[Life of a Turn](/docs/walkthrough/life-of-a-turn) traced Maya's message across 16 hops and ended
with two it called "the source of truth" and "replay, then tail" (steps 14 and 15). This page slows
those two hops down. It is the story of how events get from the server to her screen, and why a
dropped connection is a non-event.

<Callout title="You will learn">
- Why the **durable log** — not the browser, not the connection — is the one source of truth.
- What `LISTEN/NOTIFY` is, in plain terms, and why it means **no Redis** and works with many servers.
- Why reconnecting is not a special path — it is just reopening the stream with `after = last sequence seen`.
- How a turn can **generate on one server** but be **streamed by any server**.
- What happens when the log was pruned, and how the "generating" dot on your other chats rides the same rails.
</Callout>

## The durable log is the source of truth

Picture a shared notebook with numbered lines. Every time the model produces something — a word, a
reasoning step, the `jira.search_issues` call, the final answer — the server writes one numbered line
into the notebook. The browser never writes to the notebook. It only ever **reads** it. That notebook
is the durable log, and it is the single fact everything else defers to.

This flips the obvious design. Your instinct is that the answer "lives" in the connection streaming
it to Maya — that the bytes on the wire *are* the answer. Here, the bytes on the wire are a **copy**.
The real answer is the rows in the log. The connection is a window onto the notebook, and you can
close and reopen a window without touching what is written on the page.

**How it's wired.** The notebook is the `turn_events` table (`packages/db`). Each `sidechat.v1` event
carries a monotonic `sequence` — line 0, 1, 2, … — and the generating fiber appends each one with
`appendStreamEvent` (step 14 in *Life of a Turn*). Two properties make the append trustworthy:

- **Append-and-notify in one transaction.** The `INSERT` and the "new rows exist" signal commit
  together. There is never a signal pointing at a row that did not get written, and never a written
  row with no signal.
- **The append is idempotent.** Writing the same sequence twice is a no-op (`ON CONFLICT DO NOTHING`
  on the sequence key), so a retry can never create a duplicate line 7.

**What breaks without it.** If the answer lived only in the connection — the old response-owned
design — then closing the laptop *is* losing the answer. There is nothing to come back to. Maya's
reload would orphan a half-finished turn and she would have to ask again. The durable log is the
entire reason "leave and come back" is possible.

## LISTEN/NOTIFY: a doorbell, not a delivery

If the log is the truth, the server still needs to know *when* a new line was written so it can push
it to Maya without delay. That is what Postgres `LISTEN/NOTIFY` does, and the cleanest way to picture
it is a **doorbell**.

A `NOTIFY` is a doorbell that rings and says, in effect, "new rows exist for turn X." It carries no
real payload you trust — it is just a chime. Everyone who is listening hears the chime and does the
same thing in response: **go re-read the notebook**. The doorbell is not the answer. It only tells
you to go look at the page, and the page is where the truth is.

This distinction is the whole design. Because the **read** is the source of truth and the doorbell is
only a hint, two things you would otherwise worry about stop mattering:

- A **missed** doorbell is not a lost event. A low-frequency safety poll re-reads the log on a timer
  anyway, so even if a chime is dropped, the row is picked up on the next sweep. The doorbell makes
  delivery *fast*; the poll makes it *certain*.
- You do **not need Redis** or any other message broker to fan events out. Postgres already holds the
  truth and already has a doorbell built in. Adding a separate pub/sub system would mean a second
  place that could disagree with the log — and an extra service to run.

**How it's wired.** Each service instance opens **one** dedicated `LISTEN` connection (in
`packages/db`) and holds it open. When `appendStreamEvent` fires its `pg_notify`, Postgres delivers
the chime to the `LISTEN` connection on **every** instance. Each instance's dispatcher then wakes its
own local subscribers, who re-read the rows they have not seen yet. The channels are named in
`packages/db/src/schema-contract/lifecycle.ts`:

| Channel | Doorbell for | Read by |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `turn_events` | new event rows for a turn | the per-turn stream subscribers |
| `turn_cancel` | a durable cancel intent landed | the instance owning the turn's fiber |
| `turn_activity` | a turn started or finished | the sidebar "generating" dots |

**What breaks without it.** Drop the doorbell and you keep only the poll. The system is still
*correct* — the log is unchanged — but Maya watches her answer arrive in laggy chunks on the poll
interval instead of word by word. Drop the *poll* and keep only the doorbell, and a single dropped
chime could strand the last delta until the next event nudges things. You want both: the doorbell for
latency, the poll as the backstop.

<Callout title="Deep dive: the exactly-once gate">
Two sources can wake a subscriber for the same row — a `NOTIFY` and the safety poll — and a reconnect
can replay rows the subscriber later sees live again. So the stream is not naively "emit whatever a
wake-up surfaces." It tracks the highest sequence it has already emitted and emits a row **only if its
sequence is greater** (`sequence > maxEmitted`). Replay and tail can overlap freely; the gate
guarantees Maya's browser sees each line exactly once, in order. The stream then ends itself at the
first terminal event (`takeUntil(isTerminalEvent)`).
</Callout>

## Replay, then tail — and reconnect is just that again

A subscriber that opens the stream does two things in sequence: it **replays**, then it **tails**.

- **Replay** reads the notebook from a starting line and streams every existing row in order, so the
  subscriber catches up to the present. A brand-new viewer replays from the very beginning; a
  returning viewer replays from wherever it left off.
- **Tail** keeps the window open and streams each new row as it lands — driven by the doorbell, with
  the poll as backstop — until the terminal line.

The one knob that drives both is `after`: a sequence number meaning "I already have everything up to
here; give me what's past it." The browser keeps `after` updated to the **last sequence it saw**.

Now the punchline for reconnects. There is **no special reconnect code path**. When Maya reopens her
laptop, the browser opens the *same* `GET /chat/turns/:assistantTurnId/stream?after=<seq>` it always
opens — it just passes the last sequence it had seen before the lid closed. The server replays the
lines she missed during the meeting, then tails the rest. A first connection and a reconnection are
the **same request** with a different `after`. "Reconnect" is not a feature; it is the absence of one.

**How it's wired.** The subscription (step 15, `createTurnSubscriptionStream`) registers a local
listener, replays `turn_events` rows with `sequence > after`, then tails live events through the
exactly-once gate above. The browser persists the last sequence it rendered, so a cold page reload
restarts from the saved marker and an in-session blip resumes from the live cursor.

**What breaks without it.** If reconnect were its own pathway — say, "fetch a snapshot, then attach a
fresh stream" — you would have two code paths that can drift apart, and a seam where events fall
between the snapshot and the attach. Folding reconnect into "replay from `after`, then tail" means the
same battle-tested path serves the first byte and the millionth, and there is no gap to leak through.

<Callout type="warn" title="Pitfall: the stream is a window, not the worker">
It is tempting to read "the browser opens the stream" as "the browser is driving the turn." It is
not. Closing the SSE connection does **not** stop generation — the fiber on the server keeps writing
to the log whether or not anyone is watching. To actually stop a turn you must send a durable cancel
(`POST /chat/turns/:id/cancel`), which writes `cancel_requested_at` and rings the `turn_cancel`
doorbell. A closed window is not a stop signal.
</Callout>

## Many servers: generate on one, stream from any

Scale the company up to three servers behind a load balancer and the design barely notices, because
of one clean split:

- A turn **generates** on exactly **one** instance — the *owner*. Only the owner writes that turn's
  lines into the notebook.
- A turn can be **streamed** from **any** instance. Serving the stream is just reading the log and
  listening for the doorbell, and every instance can do both.

So when Maya reopens her laptop, the load balancer might route her reconnect to a *different* server
than the one generating her answer. That is fine. The reconnecting server reads the same `turn_events`
rows from the same Postgres and hears the same `turn_events` doorbell as everyone else. There is **no
sticky routing** — nothing has to pin Maya to "her" server.

**How it's wired.** Generation is forked onto a server-owned Effect fiber, and exactly one instance
claims write-ownership through an **owner lease** (covered next). The stream route, by contrast, holds
no special claim — it reads the durable log and subscribes to notifies like any reader. This is also
why a *cancel* can safely land on any instance: whichever one receives the `POST` writes the durable
intent, and the `turn_cancel` doorbell lets the actual owner interrupt its fiber.

**What breaks without it.** Pin the stream to its generating instance and you are back to sticky
routing: brittle under restarts, deploys, and scaling. If that one instance restarts mid-turn, Maya's
window goes dark even though the answer is safe in the log. Decoupling "who generates" from "who
serves" is what lets you add, remove, or restart servers without dropping anyone's stream.

<Callout title="Deep dive: the owner lease">
"Exactly one writer" is enforced, not hoped for. An owner claims the turn with a compare-and-swap on a
`lease_epoch` and renews it with a heartbeat. If the owner dies or stalls, a reaper bumps the epoch to
fence it out and terminalizes the turn, and any heartbeat from the stale owner self-interrupts when it
sees the epoch moved. The result: even across crashes and deploys, a turn's log never has two
machines writing conflicting lines. The reader side needs none of this — it only reads.
</Callout>

## When the log was pruned

The notebook does not grow forever. A background **pruner** deletes a turn's `turn_events` rows once
the turn has been terminal long enough; the conversation and its final assistant message stay, only
the now-redundant event rows go. This is almost always invisible — pruning happens well after a turn
finishes — but it creates one edge case worth naming.

Suppose Maya comes back not after twenty minutes but after a week, and her browser tries to reconnect
with an `after` pointing into rows the pruner already deleted. Replay from there is impossible: those
lines are gone. The stream cannot honestly resume, so it says so.

**How it's wired.** The route answers HTTP 404 `replay_expired` — a terminal-only transport error
code (`packages/chat-protocol/src/sidechat-v1/errors.ts`). The widget treats that not as a failure but
as a signal to **stop trying to replay a live stream** and instead read the finished conversation from
history (`sidechat.history`). Maya still sees her three tickets; they just arrive as a settled
transcript rather than a replayed stream.

**What breaks without it.** Without an explicit `replay_expired`, a reconnect into pruned rows would
either hang waiting for lines that will never come, or silently show a truncated answer starting from
some arbitrary surviving row. The distinct code turns "those events are gone" into a clean, handled
fork: live stream when the log is intact, history when it is not.

## The "generating" dot rides the same rails

Maya has several chats in her sidebar. When a turn is running in one she is *not* looking at, that
chat shows a small "generating" dot. That dot is not a second streaming system — it is the **same
doorbell-and-fan-out pattern** pointed at a different question.

Instead of "what are the new events for turn X," the dot answers "which of this person's
conversations have a turn running right now." A turn ringing the `turn_activity` doorbell when it
starts or finishes is enough to flip dots on and off across every conversation in the sidebar.

**How it's wired.** A separate subject-scoped SSE stream, `GET /chat/activity`, reuses the identical
`NOTIFY` + fan-out + SSE machinery (`apps/partner-ai-service`). One detail differs by design: the
`turn_activity` notification carries the full lifecycle payload — `{ workspaceId, subjectId,
conversationId, assistantTurnId, status }` — so the activity dispatcher can fan it out by subject
**without re-reading the log**. The per-turn stream is the careful one (durable log, replay, exactly
once); the activity stream is the lightweight cousin (no sequence, no replay, no terminal), because a
momentarily-stale dot costs nothing while a wrong word in Maya's answer would.

**What breaks without it.** Build the dot as its own bespoke mechanism and you have two transport
systems to keep correct, secure, and multi-instance safe. Reusing the proven pattern means the dot
inherits the same "works across three servers, no Redis" guarantees for free.

<Callout title="Recap">
- The **durable `turn_events` log is the source of truth**; the browser only ever reads it, and the
  connection is a disposable window onto it.
- `LISTEN/NOTIFY` is a **doorbell** that says "new rows exist" — everyone re-reads the log. Reads, not
  the chime, are the truth, which is why there is **no Redis** and it is multi-instance safe. A safety
  poll backstops a missed chime.
- **Reconnect is not special**: it is the same `GET …/stream?after=<seq>` with `after` set to the last
  sequence seen — replay the gap, then tail.
- A turn **generates on one owner instance** but can be **streamed from any** — no sticky routing.
- If the log was pruned, the stream returns `replay_expired` and the widget **falls back to
  conversation history**. The sidebar's "generating" dot reuses the same pattern via `GET /chat/activity`.
</Callout>
